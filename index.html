About

From atomic structure to measurable properties

My background is in physics and computational materials science. I use quantum-mechanical simulations to study how structure, dimensionality, strain, defects, and chemical substitution influence material properties.

My calculations follow a connected hierarchy. Density Functional Theory provides the relaxed atomic structure, charge density, band dispersion, density of states, and orbital character. The GW approximation then improves the electron addition and removal energies by accounting for many-body screening and self-energy effects. Finally, the Bethe–Salpeter Equation describes the interaction between excited electrons and holes, allowing optical absorption, exciton energies, oscillator strengths, and the momentum-space origin of optical transitions to be analysed.

By comparing these levels of theory, I examine how structural changes reshape band edges, how quasiparticle corrections modify energy gaps, and how electron–hole attraction transforms independent-particle transitions into measurable optical features.

Before beginning my PhD, I worked as a Junior Research Fellow at the Materials Modeling and Simulation Laboratory, IIITDM Kancheepuram, on the DST–SERB-funded project “Cost-effective and High-performance Transparent Conductors.”

Research

Core interests

Electronic-structure theory connected to physically meaningful questions in materials science.

01

Density Functional Theory

Ground-state charge density, structural relaxation, band structures, orbital character, spin–orbit coupling, defects, doping, and strain-dependent properties.

02

GW and excited states

Many-body quasiparticle corrections to band energies and gaps, followed by comparison with the underlying DFT electronic structure.

03

Bethe–Salpeter Equation

Electron–hole interactions, optical spectra, exciton energies, oscillator strengths, and momentum-space composition.
