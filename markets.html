How I read these together

Growth, inflation, and interest rates affect corporate demand, financing costs, household purchasing power, and the discount rate applied to future earnings. They help frame questions about valuations and risk, but none of them alone tells us whether an equity index will rise or fall next month.

02

My index-investing framework

Process first; tactical decisions second.

01

Keep the SIP as the base

A fixed Systematic Investment Plan reduces dependence on prediction and keeps capital entering the market across different conditions.

02

Treat corrections as optional

Additional phased buying during genuine drawdowns can complement the base plan only when cash was intentionally reserved and essential goals remain protected.

03

Judge the implementation

For an index fund, examine the benchmark, Total Return Index comparison, expense ratio, tracking difference, tracking error, liquidity, and fund structure—not recent return alone.

04

Respect the counterfactual

A timing strategy beating a fixed-date SIP in one sample does not prove timing skill. Results depend on the period, cash assumptions, drawdown thresholds, and what happened after each contribution.

A recurring forum debate

“SIP or buy the dip?” is usually the wrong binary.

India-investing discussions repeatedly arrive at the same practical issue: the bottom is unknowable in real time. A more testable question is whether a rules-based correction reserve improves the total plan after accounting for idle cash, missed exposure, and behavioural execution.
